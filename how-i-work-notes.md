Talking-it-out material for a future "How I work" section (candidate for both

## Why this section exists at all

The industry-standard per-project "case study" is dead weight — nobody reads it, and
it's a bootcamp tell. The repeatable, *how-I-work* stuff is repeatable, so it belongs
in **one place**, read once, applies everywhere. That frees each timeline milestone to
just be **work + results** — no milestone has to re-explain the process, because this
section already did.

- **Timeline** = the vertical cut. Chronological, per-project, what shipped and where.
- **How I work** = the horizontal cut. How Derek operates, extracted across all of it.

## The spine: there is no process — there are reads, and the read decides the move

The honest claim, and it's a *senior* claim, not a gap:

> "I don't have a repeatable process. I have a set of reads, and the read decides the move."

The junior portfolio pretends there's a fixed sequence (discover → define → ideate →
test → ship, the same diagram everyone has). The senior one admits there isn't, because
knowing how to *read the situation* is the actual skill — and a fixed process hides the
judgment that's the whole value.

**What changes the move** (the variables, not the constants):

- **How much is already known.** Greenfield/unknown (translating science, a brand-new
  category) needs heavy framing/discovery. "Make this existing flow better" needs almost
  none — go straight to the work. Same person, opposite opening move.
- **Whether the problem is the product or the team.** Sometimes the deliverable is design.
  Sometimes the real problem is the team has no shared language yet, and the "design work"
  is really building alignment. Do different things depending on which is actually broken.
- **Where the risk lives.** Risk = "will they understand it" → prototype + test early.
  Risk = "can we even build it" → talk to engineering before drawing. Risk = "does it
  feel right" → go straight to high fidelity, because the feeling *is* the spec.
- **Fidelity is a deliberate choice, not a stage.** Don't always climb the ladder.
  Sometimes jump to pixel-perfect on day one (the point is the feel); sometimes stay in
  gray boxes for weeks (the point is the structure). Micro-animation touches vs. site-wide
  layout aren't two skills — they're two ends of a dial you set based on where the
  uncertainty is.

The section could be told through **contrasting stories**: "here's a time I went straight
to high-fi and why; here's a time I refused to draw anything for a month and why." The
contrast is the content — it shows judgment.

## Formal frameworks — name them, for contrast (shows awareness of the canon)

Naming these earns the right to say "and here's why I don't just run one."

- **Design Thinking** (IDEO / d.school) — Empathize→Define→Ideate→Prototype→Test. The one
  everyone means by "the process." Cargo-cult when run by rote.
- **Double Diamond** (British Design Council) — Discover→Define→Develop→Deliver; two
  diverge/converge diamonds. Cleanest picture of "don't solve before you've framed."
- **Human-Centered Design** — the umbrella stance under both.
- **Google Design Sprint** (Knapp) — the 5-day time-boxed version. Good contrast to "it
  takes as long as it needs."
- **Agile / Scrum** — sprints, backlog; design's uneasy fit inside it is a real topic.
- **Lean UX** (Gothelf) — hypothesis→MVP→measure→learn. Kills the giant upfront deliverable.
  Pairs with the "results, not process narrative" point.
- **Dual-track Agile** — discovery track ahead of delivery track. The sophisticated answer
  to "how does design live inside Agile." Naming it signals seniority.
- **Waterfall** — the strawman everything reacts against.
- **Jobs To Be Done** — people "hire" a product for a job. A *lens*, not a process —
  changes the question you ask, not the steps you run.
- **Atomic Design** (Brad Frost) — atoms→…→pages. The design-systems canon.
- **Generative vs. evaluative research**; **heuristic evaluation, usability testing, card
  sorting, tree testing** — named methods reached for *when the risk calls for it.*
- Prioritization machinery: **North Star / OKRs, RICE, MoSCoW**.

**The contrast to draw:** most of these are either a *sequence* (do steps in order) or a
*lens* (ask this question). Derek carries the lenses and refuses the fixed sequence — the
frameworks are the vocabulary; judgment is choosing which one the moment needs. Sharpest
single contrast to build around: **Double Diamond's tidy diagram vs. how a real project
actually zigzags** (everyone recognizes the diamond, so subverting *that* lands hardest).

Recurring note for the whole section: Derek arrived at a lot of these principles *by doing*;
the frameworks just named what he'd already found (e.g. "5 people yields huge info" ≈ Nielsen).

## The phases — reorderable moves, not steps

These are not steps. They're moves, and **the situation decides the order.** State the
reorderability as a strength, not a hedge.

### Phase — Listen / the initial download

- Start by asking a lot of questions. Lots of listening, questions, notes.
- **Fast at the systems and goals, deliberately slow at the people and emotions.** Can read
  the system quickly — so spend the bought time on what can't be shortcut: domain reality
  and where everyone's head is at. (Opposite of junior "run discovery 4 weeks because the
  process says so.")
- Wide range of experience → picks up systems and goals quickly. But there's **no replacement
  for domain experience** and for sensing the challenges and emotions so far.
- **The emotional-tenure read:** have they been living with this a decade (too close, deep
  knowledge tangled with deep blind spots — needs pulling *out* of assumptions) or just
  started (needs help *finding* the structure they don't have yet)? Same first meeting,
  opposite move.
- Goal isn't total understanding — it's **"enough for the next phase."** Discovery is done
  when you can responsibly take the next step, and that line moves every time.
- Sometimes you can jump right into an existing interface, or they have drawings / a
  prototype. Sometimes Derek **builds something loose live while talking** to help, or
  **points at existing examples** to explore — a rough thing on screen gets a better reaction
  than more questions. (Prototype used as a *requirements-gathering instrument*, not an
  after-requirements step.)

The listening isn't rapport for its own sake — **it earns the specific permission to diverge.**

### Phase — Divergent thinking (and why trust comes first)

- Not Double Diamond by the book, but the **divergent/convergent split is load-bearing.**
- **You can't help someone choose until the options actually exist.** Converging on an
  incomplete set isn't deciding — it's grabbing the first thing that came up. Divergence is
  what makes the decision *legitimate* (not a creativity ritual — "you can't make a real
  choice from a fake menu").
- **Divergence is expensive; people won't spend on it unless they trust you.** Widening
  *feels* like going backwards — especially to the decade-person who thinks they know the
  answer, or the anxious just-started person who wants a decision *now* to feel safe. They'll
  only tolerate that discomfort if they trust you'll get them out the other side. **The trust
  from phase one buys the divergence.**

The through-line: **earn it → widen it → narrow it.** As close to a spine as it gets — but
how much of each, and how, changes every time.

### Phase — Get informed: reach experts and users

- After the initial download, reach out to a few real people. **Not the most-connected
  person — but can always find a way to a couple key people** (realty, investing, a local
  lawyer, a judge). That resourcefulness is rarer than a big rolodex: can parachute into an
  unfamiliar domain and surface the right few humans. It's how Derek gets domain *depth*
  without a decade in the domain — he borrows it from people who have it.
- A few **domain experts** + a few **would-be users**. Even ~5 people yields huge info
  (≈ Nielsen's 5-user rule — arrived at by doing).
- **Experts and users give very different leads, on purpose:**
  - **Experts** → how the domain actually works: constraints, regulation, why the obvious
    idea is illegal or already failed.
  - **Users** → what actually hurts: lived friction, emotional reality, the thing they'd pay
    to stop.
  - They pull in different directions; holding both keeps you from building
    elegant-but-naive *or* users-love-it-but-can't-ship. **Seeking the tension** is the move.

### Phase — Explore the space / take stock of the angles

- "**Most of my job is taking screenshots and organizing them into groups.**" (Keeper line —
  verbatim. Unpretentious + specific = credible; it makes people believe you do the work.)
- What are the options? Anything close? What are all the variations?
- Under the modesty it's real methodology, doing three things at once:
  - **Building the actual menu** — the divergence made visible and countable.
  - **Getting fluent in the domain's visual/interaction language fast** — absorbing the whole
    field's accumulated decisions at once (the visual version of "reach five experts").
  - **Finding the groupings — that's the insight.** Collecting is easy; the seniority is in
    *how you cut the piles* ("these six are the same idea; this one's doing something nobody
    else is"). The clustering is the thinking.
- Still divergence — get the whole field on the table before anyone's allowed to converge.
- **If the product already exists, test that too.** The existing thing is an input, not a
  given: where does it actually break, where is it secretly fine? (Heuristic eval / usability
  testing showing up *because the situation has something to evaluate*, not by default.)
  Changes the emotional read — poking at the thing they built means handling their baby with
  care. Same activity, different bedside manner depending on who's in the room.

### Phase — Separate the known from the unique; validate the engine before any polish

This is the founding-designer thesis. Three ideas stacked:

- **Separate the known from the unique.** Some work is solved-problem work — choose a font,
  a color, wire up some forms. Every project also has a set of **unique** parts: legal
  challenges, technical/scaling problems, a marketing system that needs building. Don't spend
  your best energy on the known — the risk and the value live in the unique parts.
- **Test the key value at the cheapest fidelity that can teach you.** The real key values can
  usually be **tested on paper.** If someone can't get through a signup flow on paper with a
  checklist, building an expensive web app to find that out is the wrong move. (Doesn't have
  to be paper — could be a throwaway prototype passed around.) Fidelity should match *what
  you're trying to learn*, not what looks impressive. "What's the cheapest thing that would
  tell us we're wrong?"
- **The AI shift — value moved from the making to the knowing-what-to-make.** Derek used to be
  one of the few who could whip up a throwaway prototype; now AI tools let anyone do it. Turn
  that into the point: now that making is cheap, the scarce skill is *aiming* it. **"The key
  is to make sure it's the right thing to make."** (The current, senior answer to "what's a
  designer for in the AI era.")
- **Engine before polish — the flag.** So many companies/agencies start with a graphic
  designer and surface-level polish. But **polish only matters if the engine beneath is solid
  and worth building at all.** Derek validates the engine first. "The graphics will come when
  it's time." (Keeper line.) *"This is why I'm an effective founding designer. I've seen it go
  all the wrong ways."* Reframes design from decoration-you-start-with to
  validation-you-earn-the-right-to-polish — the answer to "you won't waste our runway making a
  beautiful wrong thing."

**Convergence here = decomposition + delegation + alignment** (not "pick the answer"):
- Break the goal into **manageable pieces** so the work can be split.
- Empower each person to **focus in their most effective area** — assemble the right people.
- Everyone fans out to **get a temperature reading** in their own domain: back-end devs test
  a new database system or a unique scaling issue, the legal team gets out ahead of things,
  marketing works its system.
- **Not the time for silos.** Keep everyone working together; distill the shared goals down as
  far as they'll go. (This is the leadership signal — keeping the whole team pointed the same
  way while each person goes deep.)

**Structural insight to name explicitly: diverge/converge is fractal, not a one-time pass.**
Converge on a shared goal → break into pieces → each discipline *fans back out* into its own
divergent probe → converge again. The diamond repeats at every altitude and in every
discipline at once. "While we converge on the moment, it's time to open back up to the next
divergent phase." A truer model of how real teams work than the tidy single diamond.

### Phase — Parallel build on a real (low-fi) foundation; the system is the shared truth

The payoff phase — everything before was setup; here it cashes out.

- Send people out to **gather enough info to make smart decisions**: early user interviews,
  competitive/market research, trying the parts guessed to be hardest or most expensive, and
  prepping the system so everyone can work in parallel.
- **Low fidelity, but REAL — not high fidelity and fake.** The key distinction most people
  never separate. Industry default is high-fi *fake* (beautiful mockups full of invented data
  that collapse on contact with a real db). Instead: if we know we'll need a db + auth + roles
  + routes, stand up a **low-fi foundation that actually runs** — ugly but usable — so everyone
  can actually use it and be sure. "Low fidelity" and "real" aren't opposites; conflating them
  is the mistake.
- The **information architecture** driving it gets worked out and *proven* in the same motion.

**What the real foundation unlocks — every discipline works in parallel against the same
truth.** Once the data structures are proven and mandatory, they're the **contract**:
- **Visual designers** take what's now known to be mandatory and explore visual language /
  mood boards / style tiles **against real data structures** — which is exactly why the visuals
  don't come out clashing and forced. (Direct callback to the opening complaint: bootcamp case
  studies look wrong because the work is dropped into a *fake* context. Real data structures are
  the antidote — visuals grounded from the first pixel.)
- **Front-end devs** map the trickier UI details against a system that exists.
- **UX designers** use the real prototypes and system to explore and test with users — feedback
  is about the actual thing, not a clickable illusion.

**The culture/leadership headline: the system IS the communication artifact.** Because everyone
works on the real system the entire time, they can give feedback continuously — **no surprises,
no expensive pitch decks to explain where we are, because everyone knows where we're at all the
time.** Kills the biggest source of team waste: the translation layer between disciplines. No
handoffs, no big reveals, no "that's not what I thought we agreed on."

**Why the sequence pays off here:** "engine before polish" wasn't dogma — validating and
standing up the real engine first is *what makes the parallel work possible.* Polish-first can't
do this; there's no real thing for everyone to stand on.

### The close — it doesn't end, it settles into a rhythm (protect the boundary)

From here it just **repeats.** The same diverge/converge cycle runs on the in-progress
product: explore it, try ideas, come to conclusions, fold them back in. Not anti-ceremony —
low-fi-but-not-*broken* stages, real meetings, presentations, and stakeholder buy-in all have
their place. The point is **protected duration for each mode** — fairly long periods for each,
*not* smashed together where you're somehow expected to explore and complete simultaneously.

**The `< >` → `X` metaphor (closing image).** The diamond is `<` *then* `>` with **space
between** — diverge, then converge. The failure mode collapses that space until the two
arrowheads overlap into an **`X`**: now you're expected to explore and complete at the same
time. That's what breaks teams — the two modes fight and you get mush (shallow exploration +
rushed conclusions at once). The whole method depends on **keeping the `<` and `>` apart.**
Collapse the boundary and everything upstream collapses too: trust-buys-divergence, the
paper-test, parallel-build-on-real-truth — all of it needs explore and decide kept separate.

**Loop closed:** the opening complaint in this whole thread — bootcamp case studies look wrong
because work is dropped into a *fake* context — is *solved* by the parallel-build phase (real
data structures → visuals grounded from pixel one). The method's payoff is the answer to its
own opening gripe. That arrived on its own; it's the argument for the entire way of working.

## Cross-cutting principles (not phases — they run through everything)

- **Hardest parts first = the stability the rest stands on.** Attack the unknowns early — the
  hardest, most expensive, scariest parts — so everyone else builds on solid ground instead of
  quicksand. Anti-waterfall stated as a value: don't leave the scary stuff for the end, because
  the scary stuff decides whether any of the pretty stuff was worth doing. All the parallel work
  (marketing funnel, videos, testing in the wild, events, raising investment) only stays
  coordinated because the ground stopped moving. **It all relies on figuring out the hardest
  parts first to avoid the unknowns.**

- **Exploration must be funded with space, or people just copy.** The human twin of the
  `< >`-not-`X` point, and maybe the key leadership line: **"If you don't give people space to
  explore, they will not find anything."** Novelty isn't free or automatic — it's a resource you
  pay for with protected time and safety. Squeeze that space and people don't fail to produce;
  they produce the *competition's* answer, because copying is the default when there's no room to
  think. Giving people time to find **new and novel angles — not just copy the competition** —
  isn't a nicety; it's the only way anything non-obvious gets found.

- **Team shape: implementers + people who can "do the dance."** A team needs both fixed
  implementers *and* mobile generalists who flow to wherever the need is that week — marketing
  funnel, producing videos, testing in the wild, going to events, gathering investment. The
  needs reshape constantly and someone has to move with them. (Also a self-description — the
  wide-range person who drops into whatever the moment needs; a founding role is often asking
  for exactly that mobility.)

## Systems, teaching, and handoff (fills the design-systems / mentorship / AI-fluency gaps)

- **The design system *emerges*, sized to the team and the goal — don't start with one.** More
  senior than "I build design systems": the judgment is knowing a two-person project doesn't
  need one and a scaling team does, and **letting it form as needed** rather than imposing it
  early. Same instinct as "engine before polish" pointed at systems — premature abstraction is
  waste. Reads as restraint, which is a seniority tell.

- **Unifying insight: legibility-through-convention pays off three ways at once.** Build the
  system opinionated, conventional, and well-tested, and the *same* property makes it readable to:
  - **juniors** — a teaching surface; the work doubles as training up jr people,
  - **AI agents** — opinionated CSS/conventions are exactly what agents need to not go feral,
  - **the client's team** — they can actually run it.
  Most people treat "good for juniors," "good for AI," and "good for handoff" as three separate
  investments. It's one. (This is literally the house "any student can read it" bar, lived.)

- **Live style-guide for well-tested, state-ready UI.** A living, tested, *stateful* component
  library — not a static Figma artifact that rots. This is also the answer to "where's the
  craft?": design craft made durable and real.

- **Client training that already happened.** "Train the client's team to use the app — but
  they'll have been using it the whole time." The payoff of parallel-build-on-real-truth landing
  again: **no training *event*, because there was no handoff *surprise.*** Onboarding happened
  continuously, for free. Most agencies end with a big scary handoff; this dissolves it.

## Outside-in entry points (agency / consulting lane — coming into an org you didn't build)

Not new principles — existing ones pointed at a client instead of a greenfield.

- **Inventory / inventory workshops** = the "take stock / screenshot-grouping" phase turned on
  *their* system: catalog components, patterns, flows, tech, and (the prize) the inconsistencies.
  The **workshop** form does double duty:
  - **Collaborative, not an audit** — inventory *with* their team; surfaces knowledge only they
    have AND builds the trust that buys divergence later. An audit makes people defensive; a
    workshop makes them co-authors.
  - **It's a diagnostic of the org, not just the UI** — how they inventory their own stuff (what
    they argue about, what's undocumented, where the silos are) teaches you more than the artifact.
    Reading the team while reading the interface.
  - Canonical name to drop: **Brad Frost's "Interface Inventory"** — the front door to any
    design-system work (can't systematize what you haven't catalogued). Instinct first, framework
    named it.

- **Pilot projects** = "test the key value at cheapest fidelity" + "hardest-parts-first" applied
  to *organizational change.* Don't propose a big-bang design-system rollout (expensive, risky,
  easy to reject) — run a **contained pilot**, prove it in one slice, and the reference earns the
  mandate to expand. De-risks the idea *and* de-risks you (low commitment for them, proof for
  you). It's how you let a design system **emerge** instead of decreeing it — the paper-test at
  the adoption altitude.

> Lane note: both are pure **agency / outside-consultant** moves ("come in as an outside party,"
> "pilot projects"). reputable.agency / sheriffderek.consulting material — not the IC-seat pitch.

## Goals + anti-goals — and the bridge to *results* (foreground this)

- **Transparent shared goals, set early, adjustable together.** The key word is *transparent* —
  goals live in the open where the whole team steers by them, not in the founder's head or a
  locked doc. What makes it distinct from generic OKR-speak:
  - **A decision instrument, not decoration** — at a fork, hold it up against the goals; the goal
    decides, not the loudest person. (Where OKRs / North Star actually get *used*.)
  - **Visible to everyone** — another shared-truth surface (same spine as "the system is the
    communication artifact / no silos").
  - **Collectively adjusted as you go** — a living contract the team revises together, not fixed
    targets handed down. Threads "no direction" vs. "rigid plan"; the goal-level version of
    protecting the diverge/converge rhythm.

- **Define what you DON'T want to be.** Slightly contrarian — most goal-setting is all positive
  aspiration. Negative definition is often *more* clarifying: "we are not going to be X" cuts the
  option space and stops scope drift better than ten positive goals. Anti-goals + **brand-voice
  workshops** give a *reference to decide against* — the qualitative version of a metric.

- **THE BRIDGE TO RESULTS (don't miss this).** The stated point of the goals is "the things we
  need to make decisions and *actually measure against.*" That's the missing receipts piece,
  solved structurally: you can't have outcomes without defining up front what you're measuring.
  So this way of working is **the engine that generates the numbers** — and it earns a line almost
  nobody can say honestly:
  > "I don't bolt metrics on at the end to look good. The goals are transparent and measurable
  > from day one — that's *why* I can tell you exactly what moved."
  Reframes the whole pitch from "elegant philosophy, no numbers" to "a way of working that
  produces numbers *by design.*" It's the direct answer to the opening rant: results aren't
  opposed to process — the right process is what makes results legible. **Foreground this.**
