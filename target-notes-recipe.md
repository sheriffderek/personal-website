*For the personalized per-job site (derekthomaswood.com/[company]). Each career milestone gets a short note connecting it to that company's posting. This recipe makes them fast and genuinely good, for any posting.*

---

## The core idea

The note does the connective work **for the reader**. A busy hiring manager should not have to figure out why your experience matches, you hand them their own requirement plus the proof, in one breath. Done right, it reads as "this person went through our posting line by line," which is exactly the effort signal you want.

The feeling to aim for: *"Wow, they matched their real work to our actual needs and saved me the work of connecting the dots."*
The feeling to avoid: clumsy, conversational, "GoFundMe wants someone who... and I'm like that too."

---

## The formula

> **From the posting:** *"[their exact fragment]"* → [one crisp proof sentence from this milestone].

That's the backbone for most notes. Quote them, then prove it.

---

## The 13 rules

1. **Quote their words, not yours.** Pull a short verbatim fragment (5-12 words) from the posting and put it in quotes. Their language lets them recognize their own requirement instantly, and proves you actually read it.

2. **One requirement per note.** Pick the single strongest match for that milestone. One clean hit beats three fuzzy ones.

3. **One proof sentence.** Concrete, first-person, past-tense action. What you did that *is* that requirement. No wind-up ("As you can see..."), no restating the milestone (it's already above the note).

4. **Specificity is the proof.** Names, constraints, the hard part. "Reworked the color system when the brand broke on real screens" beats "worked on design systems." The specific detail is what makes it credible and un-fakeable.

5. **Match, don't stretch.** Only claim what the milestone genuinely demonstrates. A forced match is worse than no note.

6. **Some get nothing.** Deliberate blanks (a pure-dev role, an off-topic entry) make the matched notes hit harder. Note what maps; skip what doesn't.

7. **Distribute across the posting.** Across the whole set, cover the *range* of their bullets, don't prove the same one five times. Put your strongest evidence against their most important / hardest-to-fake requirements (the ones they'll gatekeep on).

8. **Keep one personal anchor personal.** The "why this company" entry is the human hook (for GoFundMe: the fire story). Don't flatten it with a requirement-quote.

9. **Sprinkle a "belief" variant, sparingly.** One or two notes can swap the quote-format for a book/tool/principle: *"One of my favorite ideas is [X], and that's exactly this."* Adds taste and texture. Keep the quote-format as the backbone so the page stays scannable.

10. **Voice: crisp, confident, no hedging, no em dashes.** These are captions, not paragraphs. If it rambles, cut it. Ask: did this sentence save the reader time or cost them time?

11. **Lead with your thinking, not their requirement.** The note reads as "here's how what I learned here connects," never "you want X, I have X." Foreground the project or the insight; let the match surface. The bold company name is a directional nudge (this is addressed to them), not the subject of a demand.

12. **Move the company name around.** Only the first note or two may open with the bold **Company** name. In every other note, weave it in mid- or end-sentence, so the column doesn't drum "**Company** wants... **Company** wants..." down the page. The name still appears once per note (it's the reminder the note is aimed at them), just not always in front.

13. **Weave a growth mindset through the whole set - this is the spine, not a bullet.** The posture is never "I'm the expert on everything you need." It's "here's real, relevant experience, AND I'm clear I don't know *your* domain yet, and going deep on a new one at real scale is exactly what excites me." Every job is about learning the unique domain. A senior person who's still hungry to learn is safe to hire; one who acts finished is a threat. So notes should show experience *and* appetite - the work I've done, and the bigger, steadier place I want to bring it to and keep growing. Read the actual posting and company context and think about it; don't just match bullet points. If the whole set reads as "I've already got all of this," it's wrong even when every claim is true.

## The rapid process (any new posting)

1. **Extract the checklist.** Pull every requirement bullet from the posting (both the "The Job" / responsibilities list and the "You" / qualifications list) into a flat list.

2. **List the milestones** from the master history (`source-materials/dereks-history.md`).

3. **Build a coverage grid.** For each milestone, mark the single posting bullet it proves best. This is the secret to doing it fast and well, it turns a vague "write notes" into a mechanical matching task, and it surfaces:
   - which milestones are noteless (no clean match),
   - which high-value bullets aren't covered yet,
   - which bullets are piling up (rebalance).

4. **Write each matched pair** with the formula: `From the posting: "[fragment]" → [proof]`.

5. **Assign the specials:** one personal anchor (why this company), and 1-2 belief-variant notes if a book/tool/principle fits naturally.

6. **Rebalance and scan the whole set.** Any bullet hit 3+ times? Any gatekeeping requirement uncovered? Move your strongest proof onto their biggest asks.

---

## Per-note quality test

- Does it quote their actual words?
- Is the proof specific enough to be un-fakeable?
- Could a busy recruiter get the match in ~3 seconds?
- Is it true, not stretched?
- If it's a "nothing" candidate, is leaving it blank stronger than a weak match?
